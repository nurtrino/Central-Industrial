This activity consists of 4 parts:

1. Transcription of the audio recording of the call, meeting or presentation. The audio will be supplied as an mp3 file by the user. The transcription via Whisper should use Whisper's highest fidelity setting, should identify different speakers, but should avoid timestamps and other artifacts... just ID the speaker, transcript exactly what they say, and when and if the speaker changes, ID the next speaker and continue as such throughout the document. NOTE: OF CRITICAL IMPORTANTCE!! The audio file should not leave the user's physical computer. The transcription should be done locally by Whisper on the local machine. The audio file should not transit the Web - should not go through Anthropic's API or ANY OTHER non local internet destination. These files are confidential and must reside solely on the user's machine or the local network.
2. Conversion of the transcript into written notes. THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT STEP and should adhere to the style tips below. It is important that notes generated from the transcript provide succinct representation of all key points from the transcript, do not leave out important details, and do not overly summarize. See style tips below for more. Save this transcript with an appropriate filename in the working folder. Once the notes are created, go back and review the transcript from the beginning and review the notes to check for important details which may have been omitted (in which case add them) and conversely, for inclusion of too much pedestrian chatter that would not be relevant to a reader who did not attend the actual meeting or event but who wants to learn a succinct but complete summary of the discussion.
3. Combine any visual materials with the notes. The user may or may not supply screenshots, key slides from an accompanying presentation or other relevant illustrations or exhibits via the dropzone. You must review the visual elements or files provided by the user and place them as images in the notes you created in step 2. The placement and location of the images is based on their context relative to the discussion points in the notes. If it cannot be determined where to put any or all of the images, put them at the end of the notes.
4. Provide the final product of notes combined with visuals in a window in the html page for the user to review. Also put the output in a Word doc saved in the work folder.



Follow these style tips when turning the transcript into notes:

Style tips:

* Avoid breaking the notes into many sections to summarize content. Take the important information from the transcript and convert into into the type of notes that someone who wat attending attended the meeting or presentation would take as if they were physical writing them on a notepad - proceed fairly linearly. It is OK to have a couple or a few subject/topic break-points but don't turn the document into a series of third-person summaries. 
* Use bullet points where possible/reasonable
* Important information means key facts, assertions, opinions or questions from any of the speakers. Unimportant information that can be discarded are casual chit-chat before or after the event , verbiage on unrelated topics, overused business jargon, repetition of a single ideas multiple times, etc.
* Use your judgment when reviewing the transcript and converting to notes - you want the notes to be the kind of product a detail oriented, smart and conscientious attendee would produce if they were writing their own notes while attending the meeting or presentation. 
* Keep the notes pretty linear. Avoid putting too many headers or breaking the notes into too many sections. If the topic fully changes, provide simple header indicating a new topic but don't create notes that seek to put every reference to a particular subject under respective headers.
* If some portion or all of the transcript is in the form of question and answer, then demarcate that portion as such, for example "Q: " --  "A: "
* Avoid using third person expressions like "The speaker
* Speaker labels in the transcript (e.g. "Speaker 1", "Speaker 2") are an AID for attribution — use them to keep straight who asserted what, who pushed back, who hedged, and who asked which question, especially in Q&A and disagreements (that is where the analytical signal usually is). BUT they come from an automated diarization step and can be imperfect (a voice may be split into two labels, two voices merged into one, or a turn mis-assigned), so treat them as a strong hint, not ground truth. Where the conversation makes a participant's role or identity clear (the host, a presenter, a named participant who introduced themselves), attribute to that role/name rather than to "Speaker N". Never manufacture a mechanical "Speaker 1 said… Speaker 2 said…" structure — weave attribution naturally into the linear, notepad-style notes, and only call out who-said-what when it actually matters (a claim, an opinion, a pointed question, a concession). If the recording is a single presenter it may not be diarized at all — that is fine; just write the notes without speaker attribution.
* Don't vary font sizes for any headers or sections. You can use bold fonts for formatting purposes. Avoid other formatting things like drawing lines to separate sections etc. If there is a genuine tabular chart like depictions of data in the transcript - you can organize it in table format in the notes.
* When a supplied slide, screenshot or document contains a genuine TABLE of data (e.g. a data table, a schedule, a results table), reproduce it as a real markdown pipe table (using `| col | col |` rows with a `|---|---|` separator) rather than as a long list of bullet points — it renders as a clean Word table and is far more scannable. Still embed the source image itself as an exhibit near that table (per step 3); the table and the image are complementary, not redundant. Do NOT also restate every cell of the table as prose bullets — that is duplicative and error-prone.
* Provide a summary of the attendees at the outset - if the identities of at least some of them are know.
* Provide date and event name or descriptive title at the top. Pin the date as specifically as the source material allows: use explicit dates spoken on the call or printed on supplied materials (e.g. an "as of" date on a slide, a deck date, a referenced closing day) to narrow it down, rather than defaulting to just a month and year when a more precise date is reasonably inferable.
* If there is a reasonable 1 to 3 sentence summary to create at the end, i.e. particular action items, or one or two main concepts the speakers were emphatic about - you can put that at the end. If there's nothing highly notable or no specific TO DO's - then no need to put any summary at the end.

ACCURACY & SOURCING (critical — these notes must be a faithful record of what was actually said and shown):

* Every fact, figure, name and description in the notes must come ONLY from the supplied transcript and the supplied documents/images. Do NOT add outside or background knowledge about the companies, people or markets discussed, even if you happen to know it — if it was not in the source material, it does not belong in the notes. Notes that blend in unstated knowledge are misleading to a reader who is trying to learn exactly what was said and shown.
* Do not guess attributions. Spoken transcripts are messy and a statement is sometimes ambiguous about WHICH company, person or entity it refers to. If you are not confident which entity a fact belongs to, do not assign it to a specific one — either omit it, attribute it only as far as the source clearly supports, or note the uncertainty briefly. Never attach a detail to a company just because the words appeared near that company's name in the transcript. When a supplied document (e.g. a data table) clarifies what a company actually is or does, use it to sanity-check attributions before writing them down (for example, do not credit a data-center or software company with an aviation/FAA license).
* Reproduce numbers exactly as stated or shown (amounts, valuations, dates, rates, share prices). Do not round, convert, or "tidy" figures, and do not infer a number that was not given.
* Never compute, sum, or reconcile figures yourself. State a total or aggregate ONLY if that exact total appears verbatim in the source — do not add up line items to produce a total, and do not derive one figure from others. If the line items do not reconcile to a stated total, simply note the discrepancy; do not invent a reconciled number. When you do cite a total, make sure it matches the source's own stated total (e.g. a slide's headline figure) — do not contradict it.
* Label document-only content. If a company, holding or topic was NOT actually discussed on the call/in the meeting and its description or figures come only from a supplied document (e.g. a deck or portfolio slide), mark it "(per deck)" or the appropriate source, or group such items under a clearly labeled heading. The reader must be able to tell what was actually said or shown in the meeting versus background pulled from supplied materials.
